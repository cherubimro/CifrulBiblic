## Title

**Atbash in the New Testament Passion Narrative: Cross-Language Numerical Signatures from the Temple Cleansing to Golgotha**

## Abstract (245 words)

The Atbash cipher (substitution by letter-reversal) is attested in the Hebrew Bible exclusively in Jeremiah (25:26, 51:1, 51:41), where שׁשׁך (Sheshach) encodes בבל (Babylon). Noegel (1996) identified nine instances computationally. However, no peer-reviewed study has applied Atbash to New Testament texts or their Hebrew retroversions. This article presents the first systematic, computationally verified application of Atbash to key terms in the Passion narrative, using an open-source toolkit (biblegematria) operating on the SBLGNT, LXX Rahlfs, and Westminster Leningrad Codex simultaneously.

The central finding is that פסח (Pesaḥ, "Passover," gematria 148) yields through Atbash וחס (veḥas, "and he spared," gematria 74 = 148/2), from the root חוס ("to spare, to have mercy"). This numerical signature aligns with MacDonald's mimesis-critical observation that Mark's Temple cleansing (Mk 11:15–17) structurally imitates Odyssey 22 — but with an inverted ending: Odysseus kills, Jesus spares. The Atbash encoding extends across the entire Passion sequence: the triumphal entry (בגד→שרק, garment→vine), the fig-tree pericope (שרש→Atbash=7, פרי→Atbash=49=7²), the arrest terminology (σπεῖρα, χιλίαρχος — over 1,000 soldiers for a group with two swords), the Barabbas episode (בר אבא = דבר = 206; Βαραββᾶς = 309; common factor 103 = Δανιήλ in LXX isopsephy), and the historically unattested privilegium paschale (Brown, 1994; Merritt, 1985).

The analysis suggests that the evangelists employed numerical encoding as a layer of narrative theology, verifiable through computational methods now available as open-source tools.
